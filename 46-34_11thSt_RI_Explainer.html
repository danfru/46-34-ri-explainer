Site Background
What this site is, where it came from, and what's planned for it.

The Property

Three adjacent lots in Long Island City totaling 12,450 sq ft — a former commercial/office building, a former auto garage, and a residential structure. Currently vacant and awaiting redevelopment.

The History

Residential use dating to the 1890s gave way to warehouse, commercial, and automotive garage operations by the mid-1900s. Neighboring properties historically housed iron works and manufacturing — the likely source of off-site contamination migrating to this lot.

What's Planned

ZD Jasper Realty intends to merge the three lots and construct a new mixed-use residential condominium — ground-floor commercial, upper residential — with a 3,065 SF cellar. The project is enrolled in the NYC Voluntary Cleanup Program (VCP).

What We Investigated
Work performed in January 2026 across soil, groundwater, and soil vapor media.
01

Site Inspection

Full property walkthrough January 6, 2026. An ~500-gallon above-ground storage tank was found in the Lot 30 basement — in good condition with no signs of leakage.

02

GPR Survey

Ground-penetrating radar conducted January 20–21 to check for underground tanks or buried structures. Result: no subsurface anomalies detected.

03

Soil Investigation

10 soil borings drilled across all three lots, 23 samples collected (including PFAS-specific samples) and analyzed for the full NYSDEC Part 375 target analyte list.

04

Groundwater Sampling

4 temporary monitoring wells installed to ~13 ft depth. Groundwater encountered 7.5–8.4 ft below surface. 8 groundwater samples collected including PFAS analysis.

05

Soil Vapor Probes

7 vapor probes installed at 5 ft depth at the planned excavation level. 8 samples (including 1 duplicate) collected and analyzed using TO-15 method for VOCs.

Key Findings
A summary of what was detected and how it compares to state cleanup standards.
⚠ Significant
🪨
Heavy Metals — Widespread in Soil
Lead found up to 1,490 mg/kg (state limit: 63 mg/kg). Mercury, arsenic, copper, barium, cadmium, and zinc also exceed cleanup standards across multiple sampling locations. Concentrated in the upper 4 feet of fill material — consistent with historic urban fill and industrial activity.
⚠ Significant
🛢️
PAHs (Asphalt Compounds) — Multiple Locations
A family of semi-volatile organic compounds associated with petroleum and combustion products was detected across the site, with the hotspot at SB-6. Benzo compounds detected at concentrations up to 7× the state cleanup objective. Strong indicator of historic industrial and automotive use.
⚠ Significant
🌿
Pesticides — DDT, Chlordane, Dieldrin
Legacy pesticides including chlordane (up to 520 µg/kg vs. 14 µg/kg standard) and DDT compounds detected at multiple locations. These were banned decades ago but persist in soil. Common in older urban fill. SB-3 is the primary hotspot.
⚠ Significant
⚡
PCBs — Two Locations
PCB-1254 detected at SB-8 and SB-9, up to 760 µg/kg (state limit: 100 µg/kg). PCBs were historically used in electrical equipment and industrial fluids. Also detected in groundwater at MW-4, indicating the soil impact is reaching the water table in the southeastern corner of the site.
⚡ Moderate
💧
Groundwater — Metals & SVOCs
Iron, manganese, aluminum, sodium, and lead exceed Class GA groundwater standards. SVOCs (PAH compounds) detected above standards at MW-1, MW-2, and MW-4. No VOCs, PFAS, or pesticides exceeded groundwater standards — a relatively positive finding for this media.
⚡ Moderate
💨
Soil Vapor — Low-Level Petroleum VOCs
1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene (a petroleum-related compound) exceeded NYSDOH vapor standards at four of seven probe locations. Overall VOC levels were low, but this pathway must be formally addressed in the Remedial Action Plan given the planned cellar construction.
⚡ Moderate
🧪
PFAS — Detected in Soil
PFOS and PFOA detected at three soil locations exceeding unrestricted SCOs. Concentrations are relatively low and did not appear in groundwater above standards — suggesting limited migration so far. PFAS findings are increasingly common on urban sites and will need to be addressed in the cleanup plan.
ℹ Informational
✅
No Free-Phase Contamination, No USTs
No non-aqueous phase liquid (free-floating oil or solvent product) was found in any monitoring well. No underground storage tanks were identified during the GPR survey. These are meaningful positives — they indicate no catastrophic release and a more manageable cleanup scope.

What This Means
Translating the data into decisions your ownership and design team need to make.

This Site Cannot Be Built "As-Is"

The contamination in soil is real, widespread, and exceeds the standard that would allow unrestricted construction (Track 1). Before a shovel goes in the ground for the condo, a formal cleanup plan — called a Remedial Action Plan (RAP) — must be approved by NYC OER. This is expected and normal for a VCP site. It doesn't stop the project, but it adds steps.

Soil Cleanup Is the Primary Task

The most significant findings are in the top 4 feet of soil — the urban fill layer loaded with heavy metals, pesticides, PAHs, and PCBs. This is the target zone. The planned cellar excavation will remove much of the hotspot material, which is actually a structural advantage for remediation. What you excavate gets managed as regulated waste.

Groundwater Needs Monitoring, Not Necessarily Active Cleanup

Groundwater results show exceedances, but no VOCs, pesticides, or PFAS exceeded standards — which is better than many urban sites. The metals and SVOCs in groundwater may be naturally occurring or representative of ambient urban conditions. NYC OER will determine whether active GW remediation is required or if monitoring is sufficient.

The Vapor Intrusion Pathway Must Be Addressed for the Cellar

Low-level petroleum vapors exceeded guidance thresholds at several probe locations. With a 3,065 SF cellar planned, this pathway cannot be left unaddressed. A sub-slab depressurization system or vapor barrier will very likely be required as part of the building design. Your architect should be looped in now.

The Good News

No free-phase oil or solvent product was found floating in the groundwater. No underground tanks were discovered. Contamination is confined to the fill layer — not deep bedrock. These factors point toward a manageable, defined cleanup scope — not an open-ended environmental liability.

Likely Cleanup Approach: Track 4 or Track 2

Given the nature and depth of contamination, the most practical path is a Track 4 approach — cap the site with the building slab and foundation, use an Environmental Easement to document the remediation, and record institutional controls. If concentrations can be reduced through targeted excavation to meet Restricted Residential SCOs, Track 2 becomes an option. OER will guide this decision.

Expert Perspective
What a senior consultant would tell you in the room — not just what the report says.
1

The Cellar Is Your Best Remediation Tool

The planned 3,065 SF cellar excavation will physically remove much of the contaminated fill — the layer where nearly all the worst soil results are concentrated (0–4 ft bgs). This is a meaningful cost offset. Every cubic yard of contaminated soil you excavate for construction purposes is one less yard you pay to remediate. Make sure your excavation contractor has a waste characterization plan in place before the first bucket comes out of the ground.

2

PCBs at SB-8 and SB-9 Are Worth a Close Look

PCB-1254 at 760 µg/kg in soil and detected in MW-4 groundwater is the finding that warrants the most attention during remediation design. PCB soil waste can be classified as hazardous under TSCA regulations above 50 ppm, which affects disposal costs significantly. Your contractor and waste disposal team should be briefed on this now. This doesn't derail the project — but disposal costs for TSCA-regulated material are real.

3

Loop In the Architect on Vapor Barrier Design Now

1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene exceedances at 4 of 7 vapor probe locations is a clear signal that the building design needs to include vapor intrusion mitigation from the start — not as an afterthought. Sub-slab depressurization systems or composite vapor barriers are standard solutions, but they need to be designed into the foundation, not retrofitted after concrete is poured. Have the conversation with your design team before RAP submittal.

4

The E-Designation Is Already Documented — Use That to Your Advantage

The site carries an existing NYC E-Designation for hazardous materials and air quality. Being enrolled in the VCP and completing a successful RI/RAP process is the path to satisfying — and ultimately removing — that E-Designation. Completing this process through OER gives the property a Certificate of Completion, which can be a selling point for future buyers and lenders and signals that the environmental liability has been formally resolved under a regulatory program.

5

PFAS Numbers Are Modest — Don't Let Them Balloon the Narrative

PFAS detections are real but relatively low, and they didn't migrate to groundwater above standards. On a site with lead at 24× and chlordane at 37× the cleanup standard, PFAS isn't driving the remediation strategy here — the metals, PCBs, and pesticides are. It's worth tracking but shouldn't be the headline concern for ownership when discussing project risk.

What Happens Next
The regulatory path from this report to construction-ready.
Step 1 — Now

RIR Review by OER

This Remedial Investigation Report has been submitted to NYC OER for review and comment. OER will issue a letter of approval or request revisions before the next phase can begin.

Step 2 — Next 3–6 Months

Remedial Action Plan (RAP)

Impact Environmental will prepare the RAP — the formal cleanup plan detailing what gets excavated, how waste will be managed, vapor mitigation design, and what institutional controls will remain on the property.

Step 3 — Following RAP Approval

Active Remediation

Site cleanup commences in coordination with demolition and foundation work. Contaminated soil is excavated and disposed of at a licensed facility. Vapor barriers or depressurization systems are installed per design.

Step 4 — End State

Certificate of Completion

Upon OER sign-off that the cleanup meets remedial action objectives, the site receives a Certificate of Completion. The E-Designation is satisfied. The property is cleared for residential construction and occupancy.
